											*Muscle Gain & Loss System*

				MUSCLE GAIN
	Triggered by: Caloric surplus + strenuous activity + high carry weight
	
	gain_modifier = (avg_daily_heavy_activity_kcal / 1000.0) + (avg_daily_carry_weight_kg)
	
	Hard cap: MUSCLE_CREATION_CAP_KCAL = 3500 kcal per day max muscle gain
	
	Logic:  Character must have surplus calories AND be performing heavy activity/carrying weight. 
	The surplus is converted to muscle up to the daily cap.
	Without activity stimulus, surplus goes to fat instead.

				MUSCLE LOSS
	Triggered by: Activity imbalance (sedentary > heavy) + moderate activity presence
	
	muscle_loss_factor = [avg_weekly_sedentary_minutes - avg_weekly_heavy_activity_minutes] × [avg_weekly_moderate_activity_minutes × 0.1]
	
	Logic: If character spends more time sedentary than doing heavy work, muscles atrophy.
	The presence of moderate activity slows atrophy (0.1 multiplier reduces loss).
	Pure sedentary (zero moderate activity) = maximum loss.
	Balanced or heavy-focused weeks = no loss or potential gain. 

				CALORIC CONVERSION
	Muscle mass stored as:  current_converted_value_ccv_kcal (3500 kcal = 1 kg muscle)
	Fat mass stored as: current_reserve_value_crv_kcal (7700 kcal = 1 kg fat)
	
	Deficit burn ratio: 3: 1 (Muscle: Fat) - muscles burned 3x faster than fat when in deficit

			DAILY vs WEEKLY PROCESSING
	Tick-level (short term: every hr): Handle immediate caloric balance (digestion vs burn)
	Daily: Calculate activity stimulus for muscle gain potential
	Weekly: Calculate activity pattern for muscle loss/atrophy

Unarmed & Body Techniques
Jūjutsu (柔術): The "gentle art" of grappling, joint locks, and throws to defeat armored opponents.
Taijutsu (体術): A general term for "body techniques" or unarmed combat methods.
Koppōjutsu (骨法術): The art of "bone-breaking" or attacking the skeletal structure.
Kosshijutsu (骨指術): Techniques focused on attacking vital points and muscle structures. 

Weapon-Based Techniques
Kenjutsu (剣術): Traditional Japanese swordsmanship focused on actual combat.
Iaijutsu (抜刀術): The art of the "sudden draw"—drawing the sword and striking in a single motion.
Sōjutsu (槍術): The art of the spear (yari).
Bōjutsu (棒術): Staff-fighting techniques using a long pole.
Naginatajutsu (長刀術): The art of the pole-arm or glaive.
Shurikenjutsu (手裏剣術): The skill of throwing small blades or spikes.
Kyūjutsu (弓術): Traditional Japanese archery (the battlefield precursor to Kyudo).
Kusari-fudōjutsu: The science of the weighted chain. While not a club, it uses the same "impact physics" to deliver hammer-like blows to the skull from a distance.

Chigiriki-jutsu: The science of the flail. A club with a weighted chain attached to the top. This allowed for a "swing limit" that could wrap around an opponent's sword or shield.
Gajutsū (Mace Art): Ancient techniques using the Kashira-tsuki, a heavy, mace-like wooden staff with a bulbous head, used before the Kanabō was standardized.

								Task Completion Time Calculation - " calculate - Skill effect on task"	
		
					1. TOTAL OBSERVED TIME (The Raw Baseline)
						T_{obs} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} s_i

					2. NORMAL TIME (Adjusting for Subject Skill/Performance)
						T_n = T_{obs} \times \left( \frac{R}{100} \right)

					3. STANDARD TIME (The Final Practical Estimate)
						T_s = T_n \times (1 + A)

							% WHERE:
					s_i = Time per individual step
					n   = Total number of steps (Task Complexity)

					R   = Performance Rating / Skill Factor (e.g., 100 = Standard)
					A   = Allowance Factor for fatigue/delays (e.g., 0.15 = 15%)

                 		The final formula to determine the subject's time to complete a task is:
					(T(s) = T(obs) x [R\100] x [1+A]
				Where: T(obs) is the sum of individual step-times.
					R is the Performance Rating 
					A is the Allowance Factor expressed as a decimal (e.g., 0.15 for 15%).
